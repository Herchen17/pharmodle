#1Post-ACL ReconstructionKnee
C1 Complaint24-year-old athlete. 4 weeks ago had knee surgery for a ligament injury. Reports pain and swelling around the surgical site. Struggling to regain normal movement and function.
C2 ActivityCannot perform sport-specific movements. Favours right leg, walks with limp. Struggles particularly with loaded knee bending (stairs, squats, kneeling).
C3 HistoryKnee ligament tear from skiing injury. Reconstruction with autograft hamstring. Early post-operative phase.
C4 ExaminationKnee flexion 80 degrees, extension lag 5-10 degrees. Quadriceps strength 3+/5. Hamstring strength weak 3/5.
C5 ImagingPost-operative X-rays show graft positioning adequate. Tunnel placement appropriate. MRI shows intact graft.
Explanation

Post-ACL reconstruction represents the early phase following surgical repair of an anterior cruciate ligament tear, typically using an autograft (hamstring, patellar tendon, or quadriceps) to restore knee stability. At four weeks post-operatively, patients naturally present with significant quadriceps inhibition, flexion contractures, and weakness — the surgical trauma to soft tissues, graft integration demands, and immobility during early healing all contribute to this neuromuscular deficit. The extension lag and quadriceps strength of 3+/5 seen here are typical findings at this stage and reflect ongoing motor inhibition rather than graft failure; likewise, hamstring weakness is expected given the harvest site and healing demands. Restoration of knee extension strength and achievement of full passive extension are the primary rehabilitation goals, as deficits at this stage are largely reversible with guided progressional loading and neuromuscular retraining.

#2Bell's PalsyCranial Nerves
C1 Complaint28-year-old presents with sudden onset of unilateral facial drooping and weakness over 24-48 hours. Notices asymmetry in mirror, difficulty closing eye on affected side, and mild discomfort behind the ear.
C2 ActivityCannot whistle or puff cheeks evenly. Drooling from corner of mouth on affected side. Difficulty with facial expressions—smiling appears lopsided. Eye waters excessively or feels dry depending on position.
C3 HistoryPreviously healthy. No trauma, surgery, or recent dental work. Symptoms appeared over one night without preceding illness or systemic complaints. No hearing loss, dizziness, or other neurological symptoms. Lives in temperate climate with seasonal outdoor exposure.
C4 ExaminationUnilateral facial weakness affecting forehead, eye, and mouth—unable to elevate eyebrow or close eye completely on affected side. Intact sensation across face. No vesicles in ear canal. Mild hyperacusis on affected side. Taste intact. Other cranial nerves and motor/sensory exam normal.
C5 ImagingEMG shows acute denervation pattern and reduced motor unit recruitment in affected facial muscles. Brain MRI unremarkable—no mass, stroke, or inflammation. Cerebrospinal fluid normal. No evidence of systemic infection on laboratory screening.
Explanation

Bell's palsy is an acute, idiopathic paralysis of the facial nerve (CN VII) affecting one side of the face, typically appearing over hours to days in previously healthy individuals. The sudden unilateral weakness across all branches—forehead, eye, and mouth—combined with intact sensation, normal imaging, and preserved taste reflects selective motor involvement of the facial nerve, distinguishing it from central stroke or mass lesions. The characteristic inability to close the eye and elevation of the eyebrow, along with hyperacusis from stapedius paralysis, are hallmark findings. Although the etiology remains debated, viral reactivation and inflammation of the nerve within the temporal bone are leading theories; most patients recover spontaneously within weeks to months, though early corticosteroids and eye care are critical to prevent complications.

#3Femoroacetabular ImpingementHip
C1 ComplaintMiddle-aged woman with intermittent hip pain after returning to recreational dancing.
C2 ActivityPain during hip adduction and internal rotation; worse with repetitive flexion movements.
C3 HistoryLifelong hip tightness; no prior hip injury. Recently increased dance frequency and intensity over past 3 months before symptom onset.
C4 ExaminationHip flexion passive range of motion 100 degrees with pain; positive impingement sign.
C5 ImagingMRI shows labral fraying without full-thickness tear; acetabular version abnormality.
Explanation

Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI) occurs when abnormal anatomy of the femoral head or acetabulum causes mechanical conflict during hip motion, most commonly during flexion and adduction. This patient's lifelong hip tightness and acetabular version abnormality represent the underlying structural predisposition that remained asymptomatic until increased repetitive loading from intensified dancing triggered pain and labral irritation. The restricted hip flexion range and positive impingement sign reflect both the anatomical constraint and secondary capsular tightness that develops as a protective response. FAI is distinct from other hip pathologies in its mechanical nature—symptoms arise predictably with movements that narrow the femoroacetabular space, and early intervention with activity modification and targeted hip mobility work can prevent progression to labral tears and osteoarthritis.

#4Temporomandibular Joint DisorderJaw
C1 ComplaintPatient reports ear pain, jaw stiffness, and headaches; symptoms worse in morning.
C2 ActivityPain provoked by talking and chewing; morning symptoms suggest nocturnal grinding.
C3 HistoryHistory of psychological stress and parafunctional habits; associated neck muscle tension and frequent tension-type headaches; no prior jaw surgery or significant dental work.
C4 ExaminationJaw deviation on opening, severe masticatory muscle tenderness, tooth wear visible, limited mouth opening.
C5 ImagingMRI shows normal disc position with early degenerative joint surface changes; EMG demonstrates sustained nocturnal masseter and temporalis hyperactivity consistent with parafunctional grinding activity.
Explanation

Temporomandibular joint disorder (TMD) arises from dysfunction of the jaw joint and its surrounding musculature, often triggered or perpetuated by stress-related parafunctional habits such as clenching and grinding. The nocturnal grinding pattern evident on EMG, combined with morning jaw stiffness and tooth wear, reflects sustained muscular hyperactivity during sleep—a hallmark of TMD in psychologically stressed individuals. The jaw deviation on opening and masticatory muscle tenderness indicate compensatory movement patterns and myofascial involvement, which frequently extend to the cervical musculature, explaining the associated neck tension and tension-type headaches. Early degenerative changes on imaging suggest that chronic mechanical overloading from parafunctional activity is beginning to stress the joint surfaces, making stress management and habit modification central to preventing progression.

#5Overtraining SyndromeSystemic
C1 ComplaintYoung female athlete reports persistent fatigue, mood changes, and recurrent lower limb injuries over past 6 months despite continued training.
C2 ActivityUnable to maintain usual training intensity; reports disproportionate fatigue on exertion and slow recovery between sessions despite high motivation.
C3 HistoryTraining volume increased 40% over 3 months; coincided with deliberate caloric restriction for competition. Multiple stress fractures in past 4 months. No previous injury history.
C4 ExaminationHR and BP lower than expected for age; slow resting heart rate. Decreased muscle bulk in legs. Generalized weakness on manual muscle testing (4/5). Menarche at age 14, normal cycle until 8 months ago.
C5 ImagingDEXA scan: T-score −2.5 spine, −2.1 femoral neck (osteopenia range). Serum 25-OH vitamin D 18 ng/mL, iron studies low-normal, TSH elevated at 5.2 mIU/L.
Explanation

Overtraining syndrome (OTS) represents a maladaptive physiological response to excessive training stress without adequate recovery, characterized by persistent underperformance, fatigue disproportionate to training load, and systemic metabolic dysfunction. This condition arises from a combination of excessive volume, insufficient nutrition, and psychological stress — in this case, the 40% training increase coupled with caloric restriction created a severe energy deficit that triggered suppression of the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis, evidenced by amenorrhea and the constellation of low bone mineral density, hypothyroidism, and low-normal iron stores collectively termed Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). The bilateral stress fractures, generalized weakness, bradycardia, and mood disturbance reflect the systemic nature of the condition, where the body has shifted into a catabolic state prioritizing survival over athletic performance. Recovery requires a holistic approach: training reduction, energy availability restoration, micronutrient repletion, and psychological support to restore hormonal homeostasis.

#6Heart FailureHeart
C1 Complaint55-year-old reports progressive shortness of breath over 4 months. Initially noticed only with vigorous exercise; now occurs with normal daily activities. Also reports unusual fatigue and occasional ankle swelling by evening.
C2 ActivityCannot climb stairs without stopping to catch breath. Struggles to walk briskly on level ground. Swelling in ankles improves somewhat with leg elevation and overnight rest. Symptoms worse in evenings and when lying flat.
C3 HistoryTreated for breast cancer 18 months ago with doxorubicin-based chemotherapy (4 cycles completed). No prior cardiac symptoms or hypertension. Recent weight gain of 3 kg over 2 months despite stable diet. No chest pain or palpitations.
C4 ExaminationElevated jugular venous pressure (6 cm H₂O). Bilateral lower limb pitting edema to mid-shin. Displaced apex beat (6th intercostal space). Quiet S3 gallop at apex. Mild bilateral crackles at lung bases. Hepatojugular reflux present.
C5 ImagingEchocardiography shows reduced left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF 38%). Dilated left ventricle (LVEDV 145 mL/m²). Grades of diastolic dysfunction. Chest X-ray demonstrates bilateral pulmonary edema with increased interstitial markings and mild cardiomegaly.
Explanation

Heart failure results from the heart's inability to pump sufficient blood to meet the body's metabolic demands, leading to systemic and pulmonary congestion. This patient's reduced ejection fraction (38%) and dilated left ventricle reflect dilated cardiomyopathy — likely chemotherapy-induced, as doxorubicin is a known cardiotoxic agent that damages myocardial contractility months to years after exposure. The clinical picture of progressive dyspnoea, orthopnoea, peripheral oedema, and elevated jugular venous pressure, combined with signs of pulmonary congestion (crackles, bilateral edema on imaging) and forward flow impairment (fatigue, exercise intolerance), is pathognomonic for left-sided heart failure with systemic venous backup. The S3 gallop and displaced apex beat further confirm the dilated, poorly contracting ventricle struggling to maintain adequate cardiac output.

#7Femoral Stress FractureHip
C1 ComplaintDistance runner reports groin or anterior hip pain that worsens during long runs.
C2 ActivityUnable to run without severe limp; pain worse with impact activities. Antalgic gait restricts hip extension and loading.
C3 HistorySignificant training volume increase, female athlete with energy deficiency. Menarche delayed or amenorrhea.
C4 ExaminationGroin pain with passive hip flexion, adduction, and internal rotation (FADI). Anterior hip pain with hip extension and axial loading. Hip abductor weakness noted. Positive hop test reproduction of groin pain.
C5 ImagingX-rays may appear normal initially, CT or MRI shows stress fracture on femoral neck. Compression-side fractures seen.
Explanation

Femoral stress fractures are incomplete fractures of the femoral neck or intertrochanteric region resulting from repetitive submaximal loading that overwhelms bone remodeling capacity, particularly in female athletes with relative energy deficiency and hormonal disruption. The characteristic anterior hip and groin pain that worsens with running reflects the tension and compression forces across the femoral neck during weight-bearing, while the positive hop test and pain with hip extension and axial loading directly provoke these mechanical stresses. Delayed menarche or amenorrhea, as seen in this distance runner, represents a critical risk factor—the hypoestrogenic state impairs bone mineral density and slows remodeling, transforming normal training stress into a fracture risk. Compression-side fractures on the medial femoral neck are particularly concerning due to their poor blood supply; early detection on MRI or CT is essential, as these can progress to displacement without appropriate load management.

#8Vertebral Compression FractureSpine
C1 ComplaintOlder adult with insidious progressive back pain and progressive height loss.
C2 ActivityUnable to maintain upright posture for prolonged periods; stooping and kyphotic positioning for comfort; difficulty walking distances due to postural fatigue.
C3 HistoryMultiple previous compression fractures, chronic condition, and associated with corticosteroid use.
C4 ExaminationIncreased thoracic kyphosis with fixed deformity; acute midline tenderness over multiple thoracic vertebrae; no neurological deficit; pain reproduced on axial loading.
C5 ImagingImaging shows multiple level compression fractures, significant kyphotic deformity, and dEXA scan confirms osteoporosis.
Explanation

Vertebral compression fractures result from collapse of the vertebral body due to insufficient bone mineral density, most commonly in the thoracic spine of older adults with osteoporosis. The insidious onset and progressive height loss reflect cumulative microfracturing and anterior wedging of vertebrae, while the development of fixed kyphotic deformity — apparent in the increased thoracic curve and stooped posture — is characteristic of advanced disease with multiple level involvement. Chronic corticosteroid use accelerates bone loss through suppression of osteoblast activity and increased osteoclast resorption, making this population particularly vulnerable to recurrent fractures. The absence of neurological deficit and reproduction of pain on axial loading help distinguish uncomplicated compression fractures from those with retropulsion causing cord compromise.

#9Cervical Spinal Cord InjuryCervical Spine
C1 Complaint72-year-old with sudden onset weakness and loss of sensation following a fall from standing height. Acute onset of limb heaviness and numbness.
C2 ActivityUnable to move arms or legs voluntarily. Cannot grip objects, lift head off pillow, or roll in bed. Difficulty breathing deeply; respiratory effort appears shallow.
C3 HistoryFall occurred 2 hours ago without protective mechanism. Known osteoporosis. No prior spinal surgery, trauma, or neurological disease. Immediate paralysis from moment of impact.
C4 ExaminationFlaccid paralysis all four limbs. Complete loss of motor power below C5 level. Sensory level: pinprick and light touch loss below shoulders; preserved sensation at face and neck. Upper trapezius weak but functional. Lower extremity reflexes absent; biceps reflexes present and brisk. Respiratory: shallow breath sounds, paradoxical abdominal breathing pattern.
C5 ImagingMRI cervical spine: fracture-dislocation at C4-C5 with posterior ligamentous disruption. Spinal cord shows T2 hyperintensity from C4 through C6 indicating acute cord edema and contusion. No hemorrhage. Alignment severely disrupted with cord compression.
Explanation

Cervical spinal cord injury at the C4-C5 level represents acute disruption of neural transmission in the cervical enlargement, typically from traumatic mechanism such as fracture-dislocation with severe compression. The sudden onset flaccid paralysis below the shoulders with preserved upper trapezius function reflects a motor level at C5, where the uppermost cervical roots still retain innervation to proximal shoulder stabilizers. The characteristic sensory level at the shoulder, combined with the paradoxical abdominal breathing pattern—indicating loss of lower intercostal and abdominal muscle control while diaphragmatic innervation (C3-5) remains intact—are hallmark signs of mid-cervical cord damage. The T2 hyperintensity on imaging reflects acute cord edema and contusion; prognosis depends critically on the degree of structural sparing and timing of decompression, making immediate immobilization and specialist evaluation essential.

#10Post-ACL ReconstructionKnee
C1 Complaint24-year-old athlete with acute knee pain and swelling. Onset 4 weeks ago after injury during skiing. Unable to tolerate walking and light activities without discomfort.
C2 ActivityDifficulty with dynamic control: cutting and pivoting movements cause sense of instability or 'giving way'. Single-leg stance poor.
C3 HistoryInjury mechanism: hyperextension and sudden deceleration during skiing. Treated surgically with graft reconstruction 4 weeks ago. No prior knee surgery or ligament injury history.
C4 ExaminationKnee flexion 125 degrees with soft end-feel, full extension achieved. Quadriceps 4+/5, hamstring 4/5. Anterior drawer test 1+ with firm endpoint. Lachman test mildly positive with firm endpoint. No effusion on palpation.
C5 ImagingMRI shows intact patellar tendon graft with good incorporation. Knee shows normal alignment. No meniscal involvement.
Explanation

Post-ACL reconstruction represents the immediate post-operative phase following surgical graft placement to restore anterior knee stability, typically using autograft (patellar tendon, hamstring) or allograft material. At 4 weeks post-surgery, the graft is in early incorporation but remains biomechanically vulnerable; functional recovery requires progressive neuromuscular re-education, particularly proprioception and dynamic stabiliser control. The persistent difficulty with cutting and pivoting, poor single-leg stance, and residual strength deficits (quadriceps 4+/5) reflect the normal post-operative timeline, though the mild positive Lachman test with firm endpoint suggests the graft is beginning to function appropriately. Rehabilitation success depends on systematically restoring range of motion, muscular strength, and sport-specific control before return to high-demand activities — premature loading risks graft failure and re-injury.
