#1 Metformin drug id easy Endocrine
CLUES
1
Clinical Context
This oral medication is the recommended initial pharmacotherapy for a common metabolic condition affecting over one million internationals, typically initiated alongside lifestyle modifications at diagnosis.
2
Key Feature
Unlike most other agents for this condition, this drug does not stimulate insulin secretion and carries negligible risk of hypoglycaemia when used alone. It is also associated with modest weight loss rather than weight gain.
3
Pharmacology Clue
Its primary mechanism involves reducing hepatic glucose output by activating AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK). It also improves peripheral insulin sensitivity in skeletal muscle.
4
Narrowing Detail
A rare but serious adverse effect is lactic acidosis, particularly in patients with renal impairment, sepsis, or conditions causing tissue hypoxia. It must be withheld before iodinated contrast procedures.
5
Identifying Detail
This biguanide was derived from the French lilac plant (Galega officinalis) and is typically initiated at 500 mg once daily with food, titrated to a maximum of 1 g twice daily. GI side effects are minimised with the extended-release formulation.
WALKTHROUGH
Metformin's origins in the French lilac plant—a botanical ancestry uncommon among modern antidiabetic drugs—hint at its ancient roots as a natural remedy eventually refined into the world's most prescribed diabetes medication. As the recommended first-line oral agent for type 2 diabetes, its mechanism of AMPK activation immediately distinguishes it from insulin secretagogues like sulfonylureas, which carry hypoglycaemia risk and cause weight gain; metformin instead reduces hepatic glucose output while promoting modest weight loss, making it metabolically favorable even as monotherapy. The peripheral improvement in insulin sensitivity adds a secondary beneficial effect. Its defining safety concern—lactic acidosis in renal impairment, sepsis, or tissue hypoxia—combined with the mandatory washout before iodinated contrast procedures, represents a unique pharmacovigilance footprint. The extended-release formulation mitigates gastrointestinal side effects that plague the immediate-release tablet. That metformin remains the sole biguanide in active clinical use, with a dosing regimen starting at 500 mg daily and titrating to 2 g maximum, leaves no room for diagnostic ambiguity once the class and mechanism are recognized.

#2 Warfarin drug interaction easy Haematology
CLUES
1
Clinical Scenario
A 72-year-old man on long-term anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation presents with extensive bruising and a nosebleed lasting 45 minutes. His routine blood test shows a significantly supratherapeutic result on a test he has done fortnightly.
2
Co-prescribed Drugs
He was recently prescribed fluconazole for oral thrush and has been taking regular paracetamol for back pain. He also reports drinking cranberry juice daily for a urinary tract concern.
3
Interaction Effect
His INR has risen from a therapeutic 2.5 to a dangerously elevated 8.1. This degree of over-anticoagulation places him at high risk of major haemorrhage including intracranial bleeding.
4
Mechanism Clue
Fluconazole is a potent inhibitor of CYP2C9, the primary enzyme responsible for metabolising the more active S-enantiomer of this drug. Both paracetamol and cranberry juice can independently potentiate its effect.
5
Identifying Detail
This vitamin K antagonist has a narrow therapeutic index with a target INR of 2–3 for most indications. Its effect can be reversed with vitamin K (phytomenadione) or, in emergencies, with prothrombinex-VF (a prothrombin complex concentrate).
WALKTHROUGH
Warfarin is the only oral vitamin K antagonist that remains standard therapy worldwide, yet its narrow therapeutic window and complex pharmacokinetics make it notoriously prone to drug interactions—a lesson this case illustrates dramatically. The presentation of supratherapeutic INR (8.1) with spontaneous bleeding in an atrial fibrillation patient on routine anticoagulation immediately points toward over-anticoagulation rather than a primary coagulopathy. The co-prescribed medications reveal the culprit: fluconazole is a potent CYP2C9 inhibitor that directly impairs metabolism of warfarin's more potent S-enantiomer, and both paracetamol and cranberry juice independently potentiate this effect, creating a multi-hit interaction scenario. No other anticoagulant (dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban) is metabolized through CYP2C9, so they would not be affected by fluconazole in this way. The diagnostic hallmark is the vitamin K antagonist mechanism combined with the specific vulnerability to CYP2C9 inhibition—warfarin's metabolism depends critically on this single enzyme pathway for its active form, making it uniquely susceptible to this particular drug combination among all available anticoagulants.

#3 Salbutamol mixed profile easy Respiratory
CLUES
1
Clinical Context
This inhaled medication is found in virtually every emergency department, ambulance, and household medicine cabinet internationally. It is the standard reliever medication for episodic breathlessness in obstructive airway diseases.
2
Pharmacology Clue
It acts as a selective agonist at beta-2 adrenoceptors on bronchial smooth muscle, causing relaxation via increased intracellular cyclic AMP. Onset of action is within 5 minutes, with duration of 4–6 hours.
3
Key Feature
In acute severe presentations, it can be delivered via nebuliser at doses of 5 mg repeated every 20 minutes, or continuously. Intravenous administration is reserved for life-threatening cases unresponsive to inhaled therapy.
4
Narrowing Detail
Common adverse effects include fine tremor, tachycardia, and hypokalaemia at high doses. Overuse of this reliever (more than 3 days per week) indicates poorly controlled disease requiring a step-up in preventer therapy.
5
Identifying Detail
Available internationally in a distinctive blue pressurised metered-dose inhaler delivering 100 micrograms per actuation, this short-acting beta-2 agonist is also used to manage hyperkalaemia by driving potassium intracellularly via beta-2 receptor stimulation.
WALKTHROUGH
Salbutamol's journey from laboratory synthesis to the iconic blue inhaler in millions of pockets worldwide exemplifies how a single molecule can become synonymous with asthma relief globally. The selective beta-2 adrenoceptor agonism mechanism—driving cyclic AMP accumulation in bronchial smooth muscle to achieve bronchodilation within five minutes—immediately distinguishes this rapid-acting agent from longer-acting preventers like fluticasone or salmeterol, which would never serve as emergency relievers. Its availability as both inhaled and intravenous formulations, escalating from standard-dose metered-dose inhalers to high-dose nebulised therapy (5 mg every 20 minutes) in acute severe asthma, reveals a drug designed for symptom management across severity spectra. The adverse effect profile—fine tremor, tachycardia, and hypokalaemia—reflects systemic beta-2 stimulation and is dose-dependent, a hallmark of adrenergic agents. What uniquely identifies salbutamol among all short-acting beta-2 agonists is its secondary indication: driving potassium intracellularly in hyperkalaemia management, leveraging the same receptor mechanism that opens airways, making it therapeutically bidirectional in ways no other reliever inhaler achieves.

#4 Lithium adr case easy Psychiatry
CLUES
1
Patient Presentation
A 34-year-old man with bipolar I disorder presents with coarse tremor, nausea, vomiting, and confusion. He reports excessive thirst and polyuria that has been present for months, but his acute symptoms began two days ago after starting a new medication for hypertension.
2
Investigations
Serum levels of his psychiatric medication are 2.1 mmol/L (therapeutic range 0.6–0.8 mmol/L for maintenance). Renal function shows elevated creatinine. Urine osmolality is low despite clinical dehydration. TSH is elevated at 8.2 mU/L.
3
Key Finding
The toxicity was precipitated by the recent addition of an ACE inhibitor, which reduced renal clearance of this monovalent cation. Chronic use has caused nephrogenic diabetes insipidus and hypothyroidism — both well-recognised long-term effects.
4
Drug Clue
This mood stabiliser is primarily excreted unchanged by the kidneys, where it is handled like sodium in the proximal tubule. Any drug or condition that reduces sodium reabsorption or GFR will increase its serum concentration and risk of toxicity.
5
Identifying Detail
This alkali metal, atomic number 3, has the narrowest therapeutic index of any psychiatric medication. Monitoring requires regular serum trough levels, thyroid function, and renal function. Toxicity causes a characteristic coarse tremor progressing to seizures and coma.
WALKTHROUGH
Lithium is the only elemental drug in psychiatric practice — a simple alkali metal that became a cornerstone mood stabiliser despite its notorious unpredictability and danger. The presentation of coarse tremor, confusion, and polyuria in a patient whose psychiatric medication levels reached 2.1 mmol/L immediately signals toxicity in a drug with the narrowest therapeutic window of any psychotropic agent. The chronic polyuria and low urine osmolality despite dehydration point to nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, a well-established long-term effect unique to lithium, not seen with other mood stabilisers like valproate or lamotrigine. The elevated TSH confirms lithium-induced hypothyroidism, another hallmark chronic consequence. The critical clue arrives with the ACE inhibitor addition: because lithium is handled identically to sodium in the proximal tubule and excreted unchanged by the kidneys, any drug that reduces renal perfusion or sodium reabsorption predictably increases lithium retention and serum concentration. The serum level of 2.1 mmol/L far exceeds the maintenance therapeutic range of 0.6–0.8 mmol/L, and only lithium among psychiatric medications has this narrow index requiring such meticulous monitoring of levels, renal function, and thyroid status. This is the drug where an innocent antihypertensive becomes a life-threatening drug interaction.

#5 Digoxin drug interaction easy Cardiovascular
CLUES
1
Clinical Scenario
An 81-year-old woman with heart failure and atrial fibrillation presents with nausea, visual disturbances described as 'yellow-green halos around lights,' and a heart rate of 38 bpm. ECG shows a slow ventricular rate with occasional ectopics and a characteristic 'reverse tick' ST depression pattern.
2
Co-prescribed Drugs
Her medications include frusemide 40 mg daily, amiodarone (recently commenced), and she has been taking an over-the-counter antacid containing aluminium hydroxide. She was also given clarithromycin last week for a chest infection.
3
Interaction Effect
Her serum drug level is 3.8 nmol/L (therapeutic range 1.0–2.6 nmol/L). Potassium is low at 2.9 mmol/L from frusemide, which dramatically increases sensitivity to this drug's toxic effects even at therapeutic levels.
4
Mechanism Clue
Amiodarone increases this drug's serum concentration by inhibiting P-glycoprotein and reducing renal clearance — the dose should be halved when amiodarone is started. Clarithromycin further increases levels by reducing gut metabolism and P-glycoprotein transport.
5
Identifying Detail
This cardiac glycoside, derived from the foxglove plant (Digitalis lanata), inhibits the Na+/K+ ATPase pump. Its specific antidote is antibody fragments (Digibind/DigiFab) that bind and neutralise the drug, indicated for life-threatening toxicity including ventricular arrhythmias.
WALKTHROUGH
Digoxin's narrow therapeutic window and propensity for toxicity — where the difference between therapeutic and toxic doses can be just a few nanograms per millilitre — makes it one of pharmacy's most treacherous drugs to manage. The clinical presentation of nausea, yellow-green visual halos, bradycardia with ectopics, and the pathognomonic 'reverse tick' ST depression on ECG are unmistakable signs of digoxin toxicity, not efficacy. Hypokalaemia from frusemide is the critical amplifier here; even at therapeutic serum levels of 3.8 nmol/L, low potassium (2.9 mmol/L) dramatically increases the drug's cardiotoxic effects by reducing Na+/K+ ATPase affinity for digoxin. The cascade of interactions — amiodarone inhibiting P-glycoprotein and reducing renal clearance (necessitating dose halving), combined with clarithromycin's inhibition of gut metabolism — explained the supratherapeutic levels. Aluminium hydroxide's malabsorption effects compounded matters. The definitive identifier is the specific antidote: digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Digibind/DigiFab) are uniquely indicated for this cardiac glycoside toxicity, a targeted rescue available for no other inotrope.

#6 Morphine drug id easy Analgesics
CLUES
1
Clinical Context
This medication remains the gold-standard strong analgesic for severe acute pain, including post-operative pain and acute myocardial infarction. It is also widely used in palliative care for cancer-related pain and dyspnoea management.
2
Key Feature
It acts primarily at mu-opioid receptors in the central nervous system. It is the reference compound against which all other opioids are compared for equianalgesic dosing — its oral bioavailability is approximately 30%, hence IV doses are roughly one-third of oral doses.
3
Pharmacology Clue
It undergoes hepatic glucuronidation to two metabolites: an active metabolite (6-glucuronide) that is more potent than the parent compound and an inactive metabolite (3-glucuronide). Both are renally excreted.
4
Narrowing Detail
Accumulation of the active 6-glucuronide metabolite in renal impairment can cause prolonged respiratory depression and sedation, making dose reduction or alternative opioid selection essential in patients with reduced GFR.
5
Identifying Detail
Derived from the opium poppy (Papaver somniferum), this is the prototypical opioid analgesic first isolated by Friedrich Sertürner in 1804. It is available in immediate-release oral solution, sustained-release capsules and tablets, and injectable formulations internationally.
WALKTHROUGH
Morphine's story begins in 1804 when Friedrich Sertürner first isolated it from the opium poppy—making it not just the oldest opioid in clinical use, but the foundational drug against which every other opioid's potency is measured. Its role as the gold-standard strong analgesic for severe acute pain, post-operative recovery, and palliative dyspnoea management immediately places it in the strongest tier of painkillers, ruling out weaker agents like codeine or tramadol. The mu-opioid receptor mechanism is shared by many opioids, but morphine's defining pharmacokinetic feature—roughly 30% oral bioavailability requiring IV doses at one-third of oral equivalents—becomes the reference standard for equianalgesic dosing across the entire drug class. Its hepatic glucuronidation produces an active 6-glucuronide metabolite that is actually more potent than the parent compound; this metabolite's accumulation in renal impairment causes the distinctive risk of prolonged respiratory depression and sedation that prescribers must actively manage through dose adjustment or opioid switching. No other opioid serves simultaneously as the historical prototype, the equianalgesic reference standard, and the drug with this particular active metabolite liability in kidney disease.

#7 Amoxicillin drug id easy Anti-infectives
CLUES
1
Clinical Context
This is one of the most frequently prescribed antibiotics internationallyn general practice, used empirically for common community-acquired infections of the upper and lower respiratory tract, urinary tract, and skin.
2
Key Feature
It is an aminopenicillin with a broader spectrum than the original penicillins, covering many Gram-positive organisms plus some Gram-negatives including Haemophilus influenzae and Escherichia coli. It does not cover beta-lactamase producing staphylococci.
3
Pharmacology Clue
Its oral bioavailability is approximately 80% — significantly higher than ampicillin (~40%) — making it the preferred oral aminopenicillin. It is primarily renally excreted and requires dose adjustment in severe renal impairment.
4
Narrowing Detail
When combined with clavulanic acid, its spectrum extends to beta-lactamase producing organisms. The combination is marketed internationally under a well-known brand name and is a mainstay of empirical therapy for animal bites and diabetic foot infections.
5
Identifying Detail
Characteristically causes a non-allergic maculopapular rash in patients with infectious mononucleosis (EBV infection) — this is not a true penicillin allergy. Available as capsules (250 mg, 500 mg), oral suspension, and in combination with clavulanic acid (as Augmentin).
WALKTHROUGH
Amoxicillin's 80% oral bioavailability made it the aminopenicillin that finally displaced ampicillin from routine practice, a pharmacokinetic advantage that immediately distinguishes it among all beta-lactams. As one of the most prescribed antibiotics globally, it covers Gram-positive organisms and several Gram-negatives like Haemophilus influenzae and E. coli, ruling out narrower agents like penicillin V but also ruling out carbapenems used for resistant pathogens. The lack of beta-lactamase coverage narrows the field away from broad-spectrum agents, yet the ability to combine with clavulanic acid into the Augmentin formulation maintains empirical power for challenging infections like animal bites and diabetic foot wounds. Renal excretion and the requirement for dose adjustment in severe renal impairment confirm its elimination profile. The clincher is pathognomonic: amoxicillin produces a characteristic non-allergic maculopapular rash specifically in patients with infectious mononucleosis, a phenomenon so distinctive it serves as a diagnostic pearl and distinguishes it from all other aminopenicillins and beta-lactam classes.

CLUES
1
Patient Presentation
A 29-year-old man with schizophrenia who failed adequate trials of two different antipsychotics presents to ED with fever of 39.2°C, sore throat, and malaise three weeks after starting a new antipsychotic reserved for treatment-resistant disease.
2
Investigations
Urgent full blood count reveals a neutrophil count of 0.3 × 10⁹/L (severe neutropenia). C-reactive protein is markedly elevated. Blood cultures are drawn. His fasting glucose is also elevated at 14 mmol/L, a new finding.
3
Key Finding
This potentially fatal agranulocytosis occurs in approximately 1–2% of patients, with highest risk in the first 18 weeks of treatment. Mandatory regular blood monitoring of neutrophil counts is required throughout treatment.
4
Drug Clue
This atypical antipsychotic is the only agent with demonstrated efficacy in treatment-resistant schizophrenia. It has high affinity for D4, 5-HT2A, muscarinic, and histaminic receptors. Tobacco smoking induces its metabolism via CYP1A2.
5
Identifying Detail
Registered monitoring programs require weekly neutrophil counts for the first 18 weeks, then four-weekly thereafter. Other characteristic adverse effects include metabolic syndrome, myocarditis (especially in the first month), constipation progressing to ileus, and excessive salivation particularly at night.
WALKTHROUGH
Clozapine stands alone as the only antipsychotic with proven efficacy in treatment-resistant schizophrenia, a distinction earned despite carrying the most demanding safety profile in psychiatric pharmacology. A patient with documented failure of two adequate antipsychotic trials presenting with fever, sore throat, and severe neutropenia (0.3 × 10⁹/L) three weeks into treatment immediately flags agranulocytosis—a potentially fatal complication that occurs in 1–2% of clozapine-treated patients, highest in the first 18 weeks. No other antipsychotic carries this signature risk or requires mandatory weekly neutrophil monitoring. The hyperglycaemia (14 mmol/L) and elevated inflammatory markers fit clozapine's well-known metabolic effects, but these overlap with other atypical agents. The mechanism—high D4 and 5-HT2A affinity with CYP1A2 metabolism—places it within atypical antipsychotics broadly, but combined with the agranulocytosis timeline and mandatory haematological surveillance, only clozapine fits. The final clincher: its constellation of distinctive adverse effects—myocarditis in the first month, severe constipation progressing to ileus, and nocturnal hypersalivation—uniquely clusters around clozapine alone.

#9 Carbamazepine drug interaction easy Neurology
CLUES
1
Clinical Scenario
A 42-year-old woman with epilepsy well-controlled for years presents with breakthrough seizures. She was recently started on the combined oral contraceptive pill for menorrhagia, but her neurologist notes her antiepileptic serum levels have not changed.
2
Co-prescribed Drugs
She takes an antiepileptic known to be a potent enzyme inducer, along with ethinylestradiol/levonorgestrel (OCP) started 6 weeks ago. She also recently completed a course of erythromycin for a skin infection.
3
Interaction Effect
The breakthrough seizures are actually not from reduced antiepileptic levels. Instead, this drug has rendered the OCP ineffective through enzyme induction, causing withdrawal bleeding misinterpreted as menorrhagia. The erythromycin transiently increased her antiepileptic levels, causing diplopia.
4
Mechanism Clue
This drug is one of the most potent inducers of CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and CYP1A2, reducing efficacy of oral contraceptives, anticoagulants, and many other drugs. Uniquely, it also induces its own metabolism (autoinduction), requiring dose escalation in the first 3–4 weeks of therapy.
5
Identifying Detail
This dibenzazepine derivative is the preferred agent for focal (partial) seizures and trigeminal neuralgia. Its major idiosyncratic risk is Stevens-Johnson syndrome/toxic epidermal necrolysis, strongly associated with HLA-B*15:02 allele, which should be tested before initiation in patients of South-East Asian ancestry.
WALKTHROUGH
Carbamazepine induces its own metabolism via CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and CYP1A2—a process called autoinduction that forces dose escalation of 25–50% over the first three to four weeks as clearance accelerates despite initially stable serum levels, making it unique among antiepileptics. The clinical scenario of breakthrough seizures after starting oral contraceptives seemed to point toward reduced antiepileptic efficacy, but the stable serum levels immediately signaled a different culprit: the OCP itself had been rendered ineffective through enzyme induction, causing unplanned withdrawal bleeding that mimicked menorrhagia. This ruling-out of reduced antiepileptic levels is critical because it distinguishes carbamazepine from agents whose own metabolism is not accelerated—a feature shared with no other antiepileptic. The erythromycin clue confirms the mechanism: a transient enzyme inhibitor elevated carbamazepine levels acutely, producing diplopia, proving the drug's susceptibility to kinetic interactions despite its own inductive capacity. The dibenzazepine structure and predominant use in focal seizures and trigeminal neuralgia narrow further, as this tricyclic-like scaffold is far less common in epilepsy treatment. The clinching identifier is the HLA-B*15:02 association with Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis—a pharmacogenomic risk unique to carbamazepine among antiepileptics, mandating allele screening in patients of Southeast Asian ancestry before initiation.

#10 Flucloxacillin adr case easy Anti-infectives
CLUES
1
Patient Presentation
A 55-year-old man presents with jaundice, dark urine, and right upper quadrant pain six weeks after completing a two-week course of an antibiotic for a skin and soft tissue infection (cellulitis). He has no history of liver disease or alcohol excess.
2
Investigations
Liver function tests show a cholestatic pattern: ALP 480 U/L (markedly elevated), GGT 320 U/L, bilirubin 85 μmol/L, with ALT only mildly elevated at 95 U/L. Hepatitis serology and autoimmune markers are negative. Ultrasound shows no biliary obstruction.
3
Key Finding
This delayed cholestatic hepatitis is a well-recognised idiosyncratic reaction to a commonly prescribed penicillin. It characteristically occurs 1–6 weeks after completing the course, can persist for weeks to months, and is more common in patients over 55 years and those treated for longer than 14 days.
4
Drug Clue
This narrow-spectrum penicillin is the standard oral agent for methicillin-sensitive Staphylococcus aureus (MSSA) infections internationally, including cellulitis, impetigo, and skin abscesses. It must be taken on an empty stomach (30 minutes before food) for adequate absorption.
5
Identifying Detail
This isoxazolyl penicillin is beta-lactamase resistant, giving it specific anti-staphylococcal activity. The characteristic delayed cholestatic hepatotoxicity distinguishes it from other penicillins and is associated with HLA-B*57:01. Available as 250 mg and 500 mg capsules, and IV formulation.
WALKTHROUGH
Flucloxacillin's delayed cholestatic hepatotoxicity—appearing weeks after the antibiotic course ends rather than during treatment—is the clinical fingerprint of an idiosyncratic drug reaction tied to HLA-B*57:01 and distinct from all other penicillins. A 55-year-old man with jaundice and dark urine six weeks post-treatment immediately suggests this delayed injury pattern, not acute hepatitis. The liver enzyme profile confirms cholestasis: alkaline phosphatase markedly elevated, GGT high, yet ALT only mildly raised and all serological workup negative, ruling out viral hepatitis and autoimmune liver disease. The penicillin's narrow anti-staphylococcal spectrum and status as the international standard for skin and soft tissue infection caused by MSSA narrows the field within the beta-lactam class. Its isoxazolyl penicillin structure—the defining chemical feature that confers beta-lactamase resistance—distinguishes it from amoxicillin and broader-spectrum agents. The requirement for empty-stomach dosing to achieve adequate absorption is clinically important but shared with other isoxazolyl penicillins. What makes flucloxacillin unique is this constellation: it alone is associated with this characteristic delayed cholestatic pattern occurring 1–6 weeks after completion, particularly in older patients treated beyond 14 days, and with the documented HLA-B*57:01 association—a pharmacogenetic marker found with no other penicillin.

#11 Paracetamol mixed profile easy Analgesics
CLUES
1
Clinical Context
This is the most used analgesic and antipyretic internationally, recommended as initial therapy for mild to moderate pain across all age groups. It is available without prescription and is considered safe in pregnancy and breastfeeding at recommended doses.
2
Pharmacology Clue
Despite decades of use, its precise mechanism of action remains incompletely understood. It is thought to act centrally, possibly through inhibition of COX-3 or the endocannabinoid system, with minimal peripheral anti-inflammatory effect.
3
Key Feature
In overdose, it is the leading cause of acute liver failure internationally. Toxicity occurs when hepatic glutathione stores are depleted, allowing the reactive metabolite NAPQI (produced via CYP2E1) to cause hepatocellular necrosis.
4
Narrowing Detail
The specific antidote is N-acetylcysteine (NAC), which replenishes glutathione stores. Treatment is guided by the modified Rumack-Matthew nomogram plotting serum drug concentration against time since ingestion.
5
Identifying Detail
Maximum recommended dose in adults is 4 g per day (1 g every 4–6 hours), reduced to 2–3 g daily in patients under 50 kg, with hepatic impairment, or chronic alcohol use. It is the active metabolite of phenacetin, which was withdrawn due to nephrotoxicity.
WALKTHROUGH
Paracetamol's hepatotoxicity in overdose stems from a metabolic Achilles' heel unique among analgesics: when glutathione stores deplete, the CYP2E1-generated metabolite NAPQI accumulates and binds directly to hepatocellular proteins, causing acute necrosis. Its position as the safest first-line analgesic in pregnancy and across all age groups immediately distinguishes it from NSAIDs, which carry teratogenic and renal risks, and from opioids, which carry respiratory depression and dependence concerns. The mechanistic uncertainty—possibly COX-3 inhibition or endocannabinoid system modulation—rules out conventional NSAIDs whose mechanism is well-established peripheral COX inhibition. Most clinically striking is that paracetamol causes minimal peripheral inflammation suppression, which separates it from true anti-inflammatory drugs when treating rheumatologic conditions. The toxidrome is unmistakable: only paracetamol overdose produces hepatic glutathione depletion as the pathophysiologic bottleneck, making N-acetylcysteine the specific antidote and the modified Rumack-Matthew nomogram the sole validated tool for risk stratification. Its genealogy as the active metabolite of the withdrawn phenacetin—a drug abandoned precisely for nephrotoxicity—confirms the molecular identity no other modern analgesic shares.

The RALES trial demonstrated a 30% mortality reduction in severe heart failure (NYHA III-IV). Its anti-androgenic effects distinguish it from eplerenone and make it a treatment option for female pattern hair loss, hormonal acne, and hirsutism in polycystic ovary syndrome.
WALKTHROUGH

#13 Doxycycline drug id medium Anti-infectives

Clinical Context

2
Key Feature
It inhibits bacterial protein synthesis by binding to the 30S ribosomal subunit. Unlike older members of its class, its absorption is only mildly affected by food (though dairy and antacids containing divalent/trivalent cations still reduce absorption through chelation).
3
Pharmacology Clue
It has excellent oral bioavailability (>90%) and a long half-life of 16–18 hours, allowing once-to-twice daily dosing. Uniquely among tetracyclines, it is primarily eliminated via the GI tract and does not accumulate in renal failure, making it safe in renal impairment.

5
Identifying Detail
This is the most commonly prescribed tetracycline internationally. For chlamydia, the standard course is 100 mg twice daily for 7 days. For malaria prophylaxis, 100 mg daily is started 1–2 days before travel. It is contraindicated in pregnancy and children under 8 years due to permanent teeth discolouration.
WALKTHROUGH
Doxycycline's non-renal elimination pathway—a rarity among tetracyclines—makes it the only member of its class safe to prescribe in advanced renal failure, fundamentally broadening its clinical footprint beyond what conventional tetracyclines can achieve. The absolute contraindication in children under 8 years and pregnancy due to irreversible dental staining, combined with the standard 100 mg twice-daily dosing for chlamydia and once-daily malaria prophylaxis regimens, identifies it as the most widely prescribed tetracycline globally—a clinically dominant position that rests entirely on its superior pharmacokinetics and freedom from renal dose limitation.

Clinical Scenario
A 48-year-old woman taking long-term medication for chronic lower back pain presents with agitation, myoclonus, diaphoresis, tremor, and hyperreflexia. Her temperature is 38.8°C. She recently had her antidepressant dose increased.
2
Co-prescribed Drugs
Her regular medications include an analgesic for back pain and venlafaxine (recently increased from 150 mg to 225 mg daily). She also took two extra doses of her analgesic yesterday for a pain flare, exceeding her usual dose.
3
Interaction Effect
The clinical picture is consistent with serotonin syndrome (toxicity), precipitated by the combination of increased serotonergic antidepressant dosing and an analgesic that also has serotonergic properties. This is a potentially life-threatening interaction.
4

This antiemetic is one of the most prescribed medications in hospitals and emergency departments, used for nausea and vomiting from multiple causes including post-operative, chemotherapy-induced, and gastroenteritis-related in both adults and children.

It acts as a selective antagonist at 5-hydroxytryptamine type 3 (5-HT3) receptors, located both peripherally on vagal nerve terminals in the GI tract and centrally in the chemoreceptor trigger zone. It does not cause the extrapyramidal side effects seen with dopamine antagonist antiemetics.

Pharmacology Clue
It can be administered orally, sublingually (wafer formulation), IV, or IM. It is metabolised by multiple CYP enzymes including CYP3A4 and CYP1A2. Its half-life is approximately 3–4 hours, and it is generally well tolerated with headache being the most common side effect.
